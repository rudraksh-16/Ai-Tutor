Understanding Momentum & Impulse

Momentum is a fundamental concept in physics that describes the 'quantity of motion' an object possesses. It is a vector quantity, meaning it has both magnitude and direction.

p = m * v
// Momentum (p) = mass (m) * velocity (v)
// SI Unit: kg·m/s

The Conservation Law

In a closed system, the total momentum remains constant. This means the sum of momenta before a collision is exactly equal to the sum after the collision, provided no external forces act on it.

Key Logic

"The quantity of motion is neither created nor destroyed, only transferred."

Example Case

Two billiard balls hitting head-on exchange momenta proportionally to their masses.

Impulse: Impact over Time

Impulse is the change in momentum. When you apply a force for a specific duration, you are delivering an impulse. This is why car crumple zones save lives — they increase the *time* of impact, which lowers the peak *force* required to stop the car's momentum.
